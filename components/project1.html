U.S. HOUSING CONSTRAINTS

GitHub Repository

Overview

In a team of four, we analyzed several factors to determine their relation to housing in the United States during the 20-year period of 2004 to 2023. These factors included: household income, population growth, interest rates, building permits, unemployment, and education. We found some of these factors are correlated while others are not. We also identified several trends in housing, such as declining home affordability and fluctuations due to the 2008 housing crisis as well as COVID in 2020.

Methodology

This project used Python (Jupyter Notebook, Pandas, Numpy, Matplotlib, SciPy, Geopandas) to read data from CSV files, conduct statistical analyses, and produce visualizations. Data was retrieved from FRED using their API.

Results

Figure 1

The change in median home values from 2004 to 2023 shows a great disparity between the top and bottom five states. The least-expensive states are closely clustered in value, with little fluctuation over this 20-year-period. The five most-expensive states, however, show much greater volatility and wider differences in values in any given year. California, for example, experienced a peak in 2006, followed by a trough that it didn't recover from until 2018.

 
Figure 2

These graphs plot linear regression between median home values and median incomes for 2004 and 2023. While there is only a moderate linear relationship (R2=0.56 in 2004 and R2=0.46 in 2023), the more important point is that this relationship actually decreased, meaning median income became less reliable as a predictor of home values. This is concerning, as for most homebuyers income is the primary mechanism for home acquisition. When income is less dependable for pricing a home, potential homebuyers may face additional difficulties as they have fewer other assets to leverage for the purchase.

 
Figures 3 & 4

Home affordability is the ratio of income to home value, and here we see a comparison of the top and bottom five states for 2004 and 2023. 2004 is unsurprising: the most affordable states are predominantly rural, while the least affordable are notably urbanized, with the exception of Hawaii (which is expected to be less affordable by its nature as a remote tropical island paradise). Comparing to 2023, we see a few important changes. First, affordability decreased across the board, even among the most affordable states. Second, Illinois became the fifth most affordable state. This is interesting because, while Illinois does have considerable farmland, it is also home to Chicago, the third most populous city in the country; one would expect that would put it toward the other end of the scale. Next, while Hawaii overtook California as the least affordable state, California's affordability still decreased; Hawaii's just decreased by a greater margin. Lastly, and perhaps most surprising, Idaho became the third least affordable state, suggesting a housing crisis in the rural state.

 
 
Figure 5

This map shows the percentage change in home affordability between 2004 and 2023. It has three noteworthy aspects:

  • The map largely trends toward red, corrobroating the previous point that in most states affordability decreased
  • Of the few states in green, Illinois shows the greatest increase in affordability, explaining its showing as the fifth most affordable state in 2023.
  • Idaho clearly has the largest decrease at around 50%, reflecting a potential affordability disaster there.

Note that Montana and North Dakota (in black) did not provide data in 2004, so they are omitted from the results.
